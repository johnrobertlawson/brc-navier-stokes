# Glossary

**Ancient solution**
A solution defined for all negative times up to some endpoint. Rescaling around a
hypothetical finite-time singularity can produce an ancient limiting object.

**Blow-up**
Failure of the continuation norm needed for a smooth solution at a finite time. It does
not mean that every weak description ceases to exist.

**Critical norm**
A norm unchanged by the natural Navier–Stokes scaling. Critical estimates sit at the
border where zooming gives no automatic smallness.

**Depletion**
Reduction of the effective nonlinear stretching due to alignment, cancellation,
sparseness, or another structure.

**Dissipation**
Viscous smoothing represented by \(\nu\Delta u\). In the energy identity it removes
kinetic energy at rate \(\nu\|\nabla u\|_2^2\).

**Enstrophy**
Usually \(\frac12\|\omega\|_2^2\). Its evolution in 3D contains vortex stretching and is
not known to remain globally bounded for arbitrary smooth data.

**Intermittency**
Concentration of intense activity into a small fraction of space, time, or frequency.

**Leray–Hopf solution**
A global finite-energy weak solution satisfying an energy inequality and weak temporal
continuity. It need not be smooth by definition.

**Lorentz space \(L^{p,q}\)**
A refinement of \(L^p\) measuring the distribution of large values. \(L^{p,\infty}\) is
weak-\(L^p\), the largest and most delicate member at fixed \(p\).

**Minimal blow-up object**
A hypothetical singular solution chosen at the smallest possible critical size, often
expected to be compact modulo scaling and translation. The aim is then to prove it
cannot exist.

**Pressure nonlocality**
Incompressibility determines pressure through a Poisson equation. A change in one region
can therefore affect the pressure field globally.

**Regularity criterion**
A conditional theorem: if a solution controls a specified norm or geometry, it remains
regular. The criterion solves Clay only if its hypothesis is shown to be universal.

**Suitable weak solution**
A weak solution satisfying a local energy inequality, enabling epsilon-regularity and
partial-regularity arguments.

**Type-I / Type-II blow-up**
Type-I obeys a scale-natural upper rate near the singular time. Type-II exceeds or
escapes that rate and is generally less rigid.

**Vorticity**
\(\omega=\nabla\times u\), the local rotation of the flow.

**Vorticity direction**
\(\xi=\omega/|\omega|\) wherever \(\omega\ne0\). Its behavior near vorticity zeros must
be handled explicitly in geometric criteria.

**Vortex stretching**
\((\omega\cdot\nabla)u\), the genuinely three-dimensional mechanism that can amplify
vorticity.
