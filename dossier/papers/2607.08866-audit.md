## Plain-language reading

The paper examines a specific possible singularity: vorticity grows at the critical
point-like rate \( |x|^{-2} \), but its *direction* becomes more coherent on small balls
by a very slow logarithmic amount. Parallel vortex filaments do not stretch one another
in the worst possible way. The author turns that geometric cancellation into a
commutator estimate, obtains a logarithmic reduction in the dangerous stretching, and
argues that viscosity then has enough room to win.

If correct, the work closes an interesting class that permits much rougher directional
behavior than ordinary continuity. It does not show that every possible Navier–Stokes
singularity belongs to that class.

## Exact scope carried by “critical point singularity”

The source definition contains more structure than the phrase “critical vorticity” alone:

1. A first possible singular time \(T^*\) and a time interval immediately before it.
2. A distinguished spatial center, normalized to the origin.
3. A factorization of the vorticity magnitude resembling
   \(\omega(x,t)=\Phi(x,t)|x|^{-2}\), with a uniformly bounded shape factor.
4. Uniform containment of high-vorticity superlevel sets
   \(A_\lambda(t)\subset B_{C\lambda^{-1/2}}\).
5. A uniform critical bound
   \(\omega\in L_t^\infty L_x^{3/2,\infty}\).
6. A uniform log-weighted mean-oscillation bound on
   \(\xi=\boldsymbol\omega/|\boldsymbol\omega|\).
7. A projected-mild velocity normalisation, or a uniformly bounded spatially constant
   harmonic background, when the Biot–Savart representative is converted into an
   absolute velocity distribution.

Items 3–4 are not consequences of a bare weak-\(L^{3/2}\) bound: that norm controls the
*total volume* of a superlevel set, not its containment in one fixed ball. This distinction
must remain visible in every restatement of the theorem.

## Proposed proof chain

### P1 — Geometric cancellation

The strain \(S\) is represented as a Calderón–Zygmund operator applied to vorticity. A
strictly unidirectional vorticity contribution cancels in the stretching eigenvalue
\(\alpha=\xi\cdot S\xi\). The remaining term is expressed as a commutator between the
singular integral and multiplication by \(\xi\).

### P2 — Local logarithmic commutator gain

Split vorticity into near and far fields relative to a dangerous ball \(B_R\). Use a BMO
extension and Coifman–Rochberg–Weiss estimate near the core, then dyadic shell estimates
for the far field. Claimed output:

\[
\|\alpha\|_{L^{3/2,\infty}(B_R)}
\lesssim |\log R|^{-1}.
\]

### P3 — Absorb stretching and improve the vorticity tail

Test the scalar vorticity-magnitude inequality against
\(\omega_\lambda=(\omega-\lambda)_+\). Because
\(A_\lambda\subset B_{C\lambda^{-1/2}}\), P2 makes the quadratic stretching term small
enough to absorb into viscosity at large \(\lambda\). Interpolation and damping are
claimed to yield

\[
|\{\omega>\lambda\}|
\lesssim \lambda^{-3/2}(\log\lambda)^{-3/2}.
\]

### P4 — Transfer the gain to velocity

Apply a rearrangement form of the Biot–Savart/Riesz-potential estimate. For the
normalised velocity, the vorticity tail gives

\[
u^*(s)\lesssim s^{-1/3}\log^{-1}(e/s),
\]

equivalently, at high levels,

\[
|\{|u|>\lambda\}|
\lesssim \lambda^{-3}(\log\lambda)^{-3}.
\]

### P5 — Sparseness outruns the analytic scale

The velocity superlevel-set volume supplies a scale

\[
r_s\lesssim \|u(s)\|_\infty^{-1}
\log^{-1}(e+\|u(s)\|_\infty).
\]

Local analyticity supplies a radius of order
\(\rho_s\gtrsim \nu\|u(s)\|_\infty^{-1}\). Thus \(r_s/\rho_s\to0\) along an alleged
blow-up sequence.

### P6 — Harmonic-measure contradiction

Convert volume sparseness to a one-dimensional sparse direction, complexify along that
line within the analytic disk, and use a harmonic-measure maximum principle to prevent
the \(L^\infty\) norm from increasing across a chosen escape time.

## Scope gap to the Millennium Problem

The reconstructed proof establishes only:

> a projected-mild putative singularity with uniform high-level fixed-centre
> localisation, uniform weak-\(L^{3/2}\) vorticity, and one global vorticity-direction
> extension with uniform log-weighted BMO control cannot occur.

To reach alternative A or B, additional reduction theorems would need to show that every
possible first singularity either satisfies this package or belongs to other fully
excluded classes. Major survivors include multiple or moving cores, anisotropic
concentration, Type-II behavior, and direction fields with no uniform logarithmic decay.
