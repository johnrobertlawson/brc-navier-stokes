The preceding theorem leaves two possibilities on the drift-feedback
branch:

1. coefficient dissipation is at least
   \(h^{-3}\exp(c h^{-7/4})\); or
2. a fixed pressure fraction is generated by a bilinear source supported
   inside the layer-coordinate ball \(B_{8h^{-3}}\).

This note sharpens the second branch. Source localisation makes the
undifferentiated pressure tensor small:

\[
\int_0^h
\|r(\tau)\otimes b^{\rm in}(\tau)\|_1\,d\tau
\lesssim h^{1/2}.
\]

The adjoint-pressure gradient is an order-one Fourier multiplier of that
tensor. Therefore its frequencies below \(K\) cost at most
\(CKh^{1/2}\). Choosing \(K\asymp h^{-1/2}\) proves that a fixed fraction
of the feedback pressure lies above the parabolic frequency:

\[
\boxed{
\int_0^h
\left\|
\bigl(I-S_{\kappa h^{-1/2}}\bigr)
\nabla\pi^*_{[r,b^{\rm in}]}(\tau)
\right\|_1\,d\tau
\ge p_{\rm hf}>0.
}
\]

On one physical trajectory at event zoom \(\sigma\), this threshold
corresponds to a descendant scale

\[
\mu
\asymp
\sigma\sqrt h.
\]

The selected physical time layer has a fixed positive normalised clock at
\(\mu\):

\[
\frac{|I|}{\mu^2}\asymp1.
\]

Thus the source-localised feedback packet cannot remain solely on the
collapsing inverse-cubic or dissipation clocks. It forces pressure
frequency at a strictly finer, non-collapsing parabolic scale.

This is not yet a nonzero suitable profile. The high-pass \(L^1\) packet
can still spread over many spatial cells or escape to still higher
frequencies.

## 1. Reviewed local-payer branch

Retain the zero-data drift-feedback remainder \(r\), the coefficient
\(b\), and the inner cutoff from the reviewed shell theorem. Thus

\[
R(h):=h^{-3},
\tag{1}
\]

\[
b^{\rm in}:=\chi_Rb,
\qquad
\chi_R=1\ \hbox{on }B_{4R},
\qquad
\operatorname{supp}\chi_R\subset B_{8R},
\tag{2}
\]

and

\[
G_h(\tau)
:=
\mathcal T(r,b^{\rm in})(\tau)
=
\nabla\Delta^{-1}\operatorname{div}
\bigl((r\cdot\nabla)b^{\rm in}\bigr).
\tag{3}
\]

On the source-localised branch,

\[
\boxed{
\int_0^h\|G_h(\tau)\|_1\,d\tau
\ge p_{\rm in}>0.
}
\tag{4}
\]

The reviewed feedback energy gives, for \(0\le\tau\le h\),

\[
\boxed{
\|r(\tau)\|_2\le C_r\tau.
}
\tag{5}
\]

The weak-\(L^3\) ceiling and finite-volume Lorentz embedding give

\[
\boxed{
\|b^{\rm in}(\tau)\|_2
\le C_bMR^{1/2}.
}
\tag{6}
\]

No global kinetic-energy estimate is used in (6).

## 2. The pressure has a small undifferentiated primitive

Because \(r\) is divergence free,

\[
(r\cdot\nabla)b_i^{\rm in}
=
\partial_k(r_kb_i^{\rm in}).
\tag{7}
\]

Consequently, componentwise,

\[
\boxed{
(G_h)_\ell
=
\partial_\ell\Delta^{-1}
\partial_i\partial_k
\left(r_kb_i^{\rm in}\right).
}
\tag{8}
\]

This is an order-one multiplier applied to the tensor

\[
F_h:=r\otimes b^{\rm in}.
\tag{9}
\]

Equations (5)--(6) give

\[
\begin{aligned}
\int_0^h\|F_h(\tau)\|_1\,d\tau
&\le
\int_0^h
\|r(\tau)\|_2
\|b^{\rm in}(\tau)\|_2\,d\tau\\
&\le
C_{\rm pr}MR^{1/2}
\int_0^h\tau\,d\tau\\
&\le
C_{\rm pr}Mh^2R^{1/2}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{10}
\]

At \(R=h^{-3}\),

\[
\boxed{
\int_0^h\|F_h(\tau)\|_1\,d\tau
\le
C_{\rm pr}M h^{1/2}.
}
\tag{11}
\]

The source is therefore the derivative of a tensor whose spacetime
\(L^1\) norm vanishes.

## 3. Low-frequency pressure estimate

Fix a radial
\(\chi\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb R^3)\) satisfying

\[
0\le\chi\le1,
\qquad
\chi(\xi)=1\quad(|\xi|\le1),
\qquad
\chi(\xi)=0\quad(|\xi|\ge2).
\tag{12}
\]

For \(K>0\), let \(S_K\) be the Fourier multiplier with symbol
\(\chi(\xi/K)\).

For every tensor component in (8), the multiplier

\[
\chi(\xi/K)
\frac{\xi_\ell\xi_i\xi_k}{|\xi|^2}
\tag{13}
\]

has a convolution kernel with \(L^1\) norm at most \(C_\chi K\). This is
the standard scaled kernel bound for a smooth low-frequency truncation of
a homogeneous order-one multiplier. Indeed, if
\(\phi=\mathcal F^{-1}\chi\), then
\(\partial_\ell R_iR_k\phi\) is smooth on bounded sets and has
\(O(|x|^{-4})\) far-field decay: the double Riesz transform has the
usual \(O(|x|^{-3})\) leading tail, and the remaining derivative gains
one power. The base kernel is therefore integrable. Scaling contributes
the single power \(K\).

Combining (8), (11), and (13) yields

\[
\boxed{
\int_0^h
\|S_KG_h(\tau)\|_1\,d\tau
\le
C_{\rm lf}K h^{1/2}.
}
\tag{14}
\]

Choose

\[
\kappa
:=
\frac{p_{\rm in}}{4C_{\rm lf}},
\qquad
K_h:=\kappa h^{-1/2}.
\tag{15}
\]

Then

\[
\int_0^h
\|S_{K_h}G_h(\tau)\|_1\,d\tau
\le
\frac{p_{\rm in}}4.
\tag{16}
\]

Since

\[
G_h=S_{K_h}G_h+(I-S_{K_h})G_h,
\tag{17}
\]

the triangle inequality, (4), and (16) give

\[
\boxed{
\int_0^h
\|(I-S_{K_h})G_h(\tau)\|_1\,d\tau
\ge
\frac{3p_{\rm in}}4
=:
p_{\rm hf}>0.
}
\tag{18}
\]

The conservative fraction \(p_{\rm in}/2\) may be used instead without
changing any conclusion.

More generally, if \(K(h)\sqrt h\to0\), then

\[
\boxed{
\int_0^h
\|S_{K(h)}G_h(\tau)\|_1\,d\tau
\longrightarrow0.
}
\tag{19}
\]

Thus no \(o(h^{-1/2})\) frequency range can pay a fixed part of the
source-localised feedback pressure.

## 4. The first non-collapsing physical clock

Suppose, as in the reviewed physical pullback, that

\[
b_j=\mathcal S_{\sigma_j}v,
\qquad
|I_j|=\sigma_j^2h_j.
\tag{20}
\]

A layer-coordinate frequency \(K_j\) corresponds to physical wavelength

\[
\mu_j:=\frac{\sigma_j}{K_j}.
\tag{21}
\]

For \(K_j=K_{h_j}=\kappa h_j^{-1/2}\),

\[
\boxed{
\mu_j
=
\kappa^{-1}\sigma_j\sqrt{h_j}.
}
\tag{22}
\]

This is strictly below the original event scale:

\[
\boxed{
\frac{\mu_j}{\sigma_j}
=
\kappa^{-1}\sqrt{h_j}
\longrightarrow0.
}
\tag{23}
\]

Its exact normalised clock is

\[
\boxed{
\frac{|I_j|}{\mu_j^2}
=
\kappa^2.
}
\tag{24}
\]

The high-pass packet in (18) may lie at frequencies still larger than
\(K_j\). Any corresponding finer physical scale has clock at least
\(\kappa^2\), not a collapsing clock.

Compare \(\mu_j\) with the interaction and actual dissipation scales from
the reviewed shell theorem:

\[
\lambda_j:=\sigma_jh_j^{-3},
\qquad
\rho_j:=\sigma_jD_b(h_j).
\tag{25}
\]

The exact ratios are

\[
\boxed{
\frac{\mu_j}{\lambda_j}
=
\kappa^{-1}h_j^{7/2},
}
\tag{26}
\]

and, using \(D_b(h_j)\ge d_Fh_j^{-13/2}\),

\[
\boxed{
\frac{\mu_j}{\rho_j}
=
\frac{\kappa^{-1}\sqrt{h_j}}{D_b(h_j)}
\le
C h_j^7.
}
\tag{27}
\]

Thus the spatial order is

\[
\boxed{
\mu_j\ll\sigma_j\ll\lambda_j\ll\rho_j.
}
\tag{28}
\]

The new descendant \(\mu_j\) is the only scale in this chain at which the
selected layer has a uniform positive clock.

## 5. What compactness is and is not obtained

Rescaling the physical Navier--Stokes trajectory at \(\mu_j\) gives a
time interval of fixed length \(\kappa^2\), with the inherited weak
\(L^3\) and local-energy bounds. Hence ordinary suitable compactness is
available for the physical coefficient after choosing spatial centres.

Equation (18), however, is a global high-pass \(L^1\) statement for an
adjoint pressure. It does not by itself choose a centre or one dyadic
frequency band. Two defects remain possible:

1. **spatial dust:** the fixed \(L^1\) packet spreads among a diverging
   number of \(\mu_j\)-scale cells inside the much larger
   \(\lambda_j\)-ball;
2. **frequency escape:** the packet moves to scales \(o(\mu_j)\), whose
   normalised clocks diverge rather than collapse.

The source support has radius

\[
\frac{\lambda_j}{\mu_j}
=
\kappa h_j^{-7/2}
\longrightarrow\infty
\tag{29}
\]

in \(\mu_j\)-coordinates, so spatial tightness is not automatic.

The original Besov detector is coarser than \(\mu_j\):

\[
\frac{\sigma_j}{\mu_j}
=
\kappa h_j^{-1/2}
\longrightarrow\infty.
\tag{30}
\]

Causal descent from the detector is proved by the exact feedback
decomposition, but retention of its pairing in a \(\mu_j\)-scale limit is
not proved.
